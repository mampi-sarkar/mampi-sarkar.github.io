Radiative impacts on the ultra-clean layers during CSET

1.      Introduction

            The marine boundary layer in the tropical and sub-tropical oceans is flaunted by the horizontally extended stratocumulus, the deeper, scattered and broken cumulus and the relatively lesser known but frequently occurring clean and thin cloud layers, often laden with precipitation. Together, these clouds impact the radiative budget of the boundary layer due to their aerial coverage, vertical extent and distinct microphysical properties. Both the stratocumulus and cumulus clouds have been studied in the past to determine their radiative impacts using observations (eg. Nicholls and Leighton, 1986; Chen et al., 2000) as well as modeling (Moeng et al.,1996; Jiang and Cotton, 2000; Zhu and Albrecht, 2003) studies, showing that the incoming shortwave and outgoing longwave radiations affect the net planetary albedo and greenhouse effect (Ramanathan et al., 1989). Together, cumulus and stratocumulus exert a global annual mean cloud-induced radiative flux change at the top of the atmosphere of -4.6 Wm-2 and -11.5 Wm-2 respectively, out of a total of -33.4 Wm-2, based on the International Satellite Cloud Climatology Project data and radiative-transfer model (Chen et al., 2000).

            More recently, the thin, low-level, liquid clouds over Greenland and across the Arctic showed a net surface melting due to the combined effect of enhanced downwelling longwave flux at surface and the increase of incoming shortwave radiation through the clouds (Bennartz et al., 2012). However, little is known about the radiative impact by the optically thinner, clean clouds with grey veil like appearances present in the sub-tropical regions, referred to as Ultra-Clean Layers (UCL). A case study is presented here to assess the radiative feedbacks from the observations made over the northeast Pacific Ocean between California and Hawaii during Cloud System Evolution in the Trades (CSET) mission in July-August 2015 (Albrecht et al., 2018). Lagrangian transitions of clouds from stratocumulus to cumulus domain were sampled while flying from California to Hawaii on the first day and then back to California on the third day (Mohrmann et al., 2019; Sarkar et al., 2020).

            UCLs were detected in the open-cell regions of the stratus layers observed during the VAMOS Ocean-Cloud-Atmosphere-Land Study (VOCALS) regional Experiment (Terai et al., 2014) with cloud drop concentration as low as 5 cm-3 (Wood et al., 2011). Low-concentration cloud samples were serendipitously observed during some shallow cloud campaigns as have been discussed in Sharon et al. (2006). The First International Satellite Cloud Climatology Project (ISCCP) Regional Experiment (FIRE) observed stratocumulus samples with 40-50 cm-3 and mean volume cloud drop diameter of 20-22 µm compared to 150-170 cm-3 drop concentration in the surrounding solid cloud decks with smaller drops. The rain rates were also higher in the low-concentration clouds than the solid clouds, which follow the Albrecht effect (Albrecht 1989) where reducing aerosol concentration in clouds can hasten precipitation. Clean cloud samples with precipitation and were also observed in the stratocumulus near the California coast during Second Dynamics and Chemistry of Marine Stratocumulus (DYCOMS-II) campaign (Stevens et al., 2005; van Zanten et al., 2005). These cases also coincided with lower downward longwave fluxes as measured from cloud-top legs. A decrease in the downwelling longwave radiation increases the net cloud-top cooling which would help in sustaining the stratocumulus layer by a top-down convection, delaying the transition of stratocumulus to cumulus clouds (Sandu et al., 2011). These cases lend further interest into examining the characteristics of the UCLs occurring during CSET with a special interest into the radiative fluxes.

            UCLs frequently occurred during CSET (Wood et al., 2018, O et al., 2018) with the total coverage increasing from less than 5% in the stratocumulus domain to 30%-60% in the predominantly cumulus domain (Wood et al., 2018). The UCLs were distinguished from their drastically low cloud drop concentration (<~10 cm-3) with large drop sizes (>1 µm) and cloud liquid water mixing ratio more than 0.01 g/kg. They mostly occur in the upper levels of highly decoupled conditions during CSET with a longevity of 1-3 hours, and are postulated to be formed by the collision-coalescence in cumulus clouds followed by the sedimentation of cloud drops leaving behind the UCLs (Wood et al., 2018; O et al., 2018).

            Three cases from three flights during CSET are chosen in this study to compare and contrast different UCL cases. These cases are discussed in detail in the second section. The third section discusses the radiative fluxes and evaluates its relationship, if any, with the microphysical properties of the clouds. The fourth section discusses the origin of the UCL cases using GOES satellite observations. Finally, the conclusions and inferences are given in the fifth section.

2.      Methods

            The sea surface temperature is obtained from ERA-Interim datasets.

            The inversion base is obtained from the in-situ thermodynamic observations from the long vertical flight legs. The maximum of the vertical gradient of potential temperature above the lifting condensation level with dθ/dz exceeding 0.5K (100m)-1 is taken as the inversion base (Stull 1988; Oke 1988; Zhou et al., 2015). The lifting condensation level is calculated using the dew point temperature and absolute temperature from in-situ observations near the surface based on Bolton (1980).

            The downward looking HIAPER Airborne Radiation Package (HARP) provided the upward and downward shortwave spectral irradiances at 350-2200 nm wavelength with a spectral resolution of 3-8 nm (Shetter et al., 2016; Albrecht et al., 2019). The spectral irradiances are integrated over the 350-2200 nm range to obtain the total shortwave flux in Wm-2. The net downward shortwave flux is calculated using ∆SWF= S↓- S↑ where S↓ and S↑ are the downward shortwave fluxes respectively. The upward (L↑) and downward (L↓) longwave radiative fluxes were measured by Kipp and Zonen broadband radiometers, spectrally integrated over 4.5 µm-40 µm wavelength (Albrecht et al., 2019). The net downward longwave flux is calculated from ∆LWF= L↓- L↑.

3.      UCL case studies

            The first case is obtained on 14 July between 134W-135W around 2100 UTC during a return flight from Hawaii to California over an ERA-Interim mean sea surface temperature of 297 K through a dominantly cumulus regime (fig. 1). The broken cumulus field is topped with thin veil clouds as observed in the GOES satellite image and in the front camera of GV. The cumulus fields are often precipitating and reaching the surface with reflectivities up to 20 dBZ. 65% of all the cloudy samples have reflectivity between -30 and 20 dBZ implying cloudy to rainy samples. The inversion base is close to the cloud tops at 1500m, limiting any further vertical growth. The estimated lifting condensation level is close to the geometrically thicker cloud bases, indicating a mixed boundary layer profile. While the deep precipitating clouds attenuate the 532 nm lidar signals, some of the geometrically thinner clouds do not attenuate the lidar with backscatter coefficients less than 10-3km-1sr-1 for example between 135.2W-135.4W and between 134.6W-134.8W.

            In contrast, the clouds sampled during the return flight from Hawaii to California on 19 July between 135.8W-137W around the same time are thinner, very lightly drizzling (<-10 dBZ) and scarce (fig. 2). The mean ERA-Interim sea surface temperature is 297 K, same as on 14 July. The lidar signals are not attenuated through these clouds. The LCL is at ~800 m with cloud base around 900-1200 m, implying a decoupled atmosphere. The cloud tops at 1200-1300 m are much lower than the inversion base at 1550 m. The moisture between the cloud top and inversion base should increase the downwelling longwave radiation and reducing the cooling at cloud top hastening the cloud break-up (Sandu et al., 2011), compared to the 14 July case. The third case is drawn from observations on 22 July between 137.4W-138.4W while flying from California to Hawaii over a mean sea surface temperature of 295 K (fig. 3), cooler than the earlier cases. Few traces of clouds are visible east of 138W at ~1 km altitude and reflectivity lower than -30 dBZ. The thin veil clouds observed in the west of 138W are situated above a deeply precipitating cumulus system at 138.4W with reflectivity of 20 dBZ. The veil clouds have a reflectivity greater than -30 dBZ indicating the presence of cloud drops. The inversion base is at 2 km, higher than the previous cases and closer to the clouds tops west of 138W. The lifting condensation level is also shallower than the previous two cases, indicating a shallower mixed-surface layer.

4.      Cloud-induced radiative effects

a.      Longwave radiative impact

            The net incoming shortwave and longwave fluxes are obtained at the top of the clouds for the three cases from the above-cloud legs flying above 2 km altitude (fig. 4). As expected, the thicker clouds on 14 July show the highest net incoming radiative fluxes compared to the other two days. Due to their lower cloud bases and more water contents, the 14 July clouds reduce the outgoing longwave fluxes. Comparatively, with an already low cloud frequency, the thinner clouds on 19 and 22 July, transmit most of the outgoing longwave radiation resulting in a net outgoing longwave flux of 130 Wm-2 and 160 Wm-2. The higher upwelling longwave flux west of 137.75W is due to the higher (~2 km) and thicker clouds with possibly higher water content (higher dBZ) compared to the traces of cumulus spikes in the east with cloud tops much closer to the surface at 700 m.
